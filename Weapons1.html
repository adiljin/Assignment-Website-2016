North Korean Video Graphically Threatens to Turn Seoul into a "Sea of Flames"

North Korea has again threatened to turn the South Korean capital of Seoul into a "Sea of Flames"—and this time has produced a video to back up its threats. Starring field artillery, bad CGI and a geriatric artillery commander, the video is backed up by a soundtrack straight out of "Wide World of Sports," circa 1979.

The video starts with self-propelled M-1977 122-millimeter howitzers and M-1989 Koksan 170-millimeter howitzers rolling into position, footage probably taken from a recent live-fire exercise near the east coast city of Wonsan. An ancient-looking artillery officer gives the order to fire, and then suddenly the video cuts to stills of North Korea's new 300-millimeter rocket artillery system belching rockets. Not much is known about this system, except that the truck chassis is apparently Chinese in origin and the rockets are estimated to have a range of at least 93 miles. The system is expected to be operational by the end of the year.

Next, bad CGI rockets are shown raining down on Seoul: first on the Blue House, the official resident of South Korea's President, followed by a number of government buildings, including the Central Government Building, Ministry of Defense headquarters and South Korea's National Intelligence Service building. Interestingly, the video doesn't depict the destruction of civilian targets, particularly the city's glittering skyscrapers and civilian landmarks.

North Korea has made the "Sea of Fire" claim for decades. The close proximity of Seoul to the border between the two countries—less than thirty miles—and the enormous number of artillery pieces North Korea has sitting on the border has led to estimates that a sustained barrage on the city could kill up to one million civilians. The threat of a destroyed capital city and massive civilian casualties has often trumped military retaliation against Pyongyang.

Still, there's good reasons to be skeptical about the "Sea of Fire." Most of North Korea's guns can't actually reach Seoul—only about 700 of the North's 20,500 artillery pieces can reach the city. U.S. and South Korean artillery and air power would be quickly shutting down whatever enemy artillery they can find, and South Korea has an excellent civil defense system with underground shelters for most of Seoul's population.

One thing about the video: the coordinates that flash on the video: 36 degrees north latitude by 126 degrees east longitude lead to an empty patch of ocean off the coast of South Korea, southwest of Seoul. It's an odd mistake—if it is a mistake—for North Korea to make. Both countries are acutely aware of their global coordinates, the 38th degree North latitude line roughly forming the border between the two.
